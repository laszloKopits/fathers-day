Feel Earnshaw

A slice through the magnetic field in the x–z plane (vertical = z, horizontal = x). Color is the potential energy of a fixed-orientation test magnet plus gravity. The white dot is the equilibrium. The whole point: it is always a saddle — a valley along one axis, a hill along the other. The repulsive plate rests against gravity but slides off sideways; the attractive overhead magnet hangs side-to-side but escapes vertically. You can move the open axis; you can never close it. Drop the magnet and watch it leak out the open axis. Click anywhere to release the test magnet there.

Every object here — the sources and the floating one — is a dipole (a little bar magnet): red end = north, blue end = south. No monopoles. The "m" in the formula below is the dipole's moment — which way the bar points and how strong — not a magnetic charge. The physics is fully 3-D (the plate is a disk of magnets, the ring a circle); you're seeing a vertical side-view slice, and κᵧ "depth" is the axis going into the screen. If the test magnet keeps slipping between magnets, tighten the spacing sliders.

Why this proves the point: the test magnet's energy is U = −m·B, and in empty space every component of B obeys Laplace's equation, so ∇²U = 0 — the three curvatures must sum to zero. Gravity is +g·z, perfectly linear, so it adds nothing to the curvatures (it only slides the equilibrium up or down — toggle gravity and watch the dot move while the ledger stays at 0). You can aim which axis is the open one; you can never close them all. The only escape is breaking ∇²U=0 — diamagnets / superconductors — which this toy does not model.
